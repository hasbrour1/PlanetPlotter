Planetary Ephemeris
Kayla Hasbrouck


Time systems and converting between dates in the aerospace world is critical. These dates can be used for planning missions, trajectories, and calculating important planetary elements. The Julian date is a measurement of time that is widely used in many astronomical calculations. Julian dates are continuous counts of days and fractions since noon universal time on January 1st, 4713 BCE6. Most calendars are different between nations, so it is important to have a universal time scale that the world agrees upon, with which one can calculate astronomical parameters needed for planning missions.

Planning trajectories to planets requires preliminary estimates to determine the mass, volume, budget, and time constraints of a spacecraft. A great basic starting point to determine the position and velocity of the planets, on a given day, is by using ephemeris estimates. Ephemerides are tabular values of position and velocity of astronomical objects, such as satellites, asteroids, and planets, at given points in time and in a specified coordinate frame2. There are many methods to calculate these values. An algorithm given by Jean Meeus is the primary method used here. The derivation of his algorithms can be found in Astronomical Algorithms, by Jean Meeus, published in 19993. Meeus isn’t the most accurate method available to find the planet parameters, but it is an easy approach for initial estimates. The initial estimates are a great start to planning mission trajectories, which can account for locations of planets with respect to one another. Relative positions of planets may influence launch windows, required fuel for launch and maneuvers, or available destinations.

Given that the Meeus method is not the most accurate method, the generated values are compared to the JPL DE405 ephemerides results, and an error analysis is provided. The JPL DE405 series uses numerical integration to propagate the planets forward in time to achieve a position and velocity output. This method is commonly used as one of the more accurate and available ways to compute planetary ephemeris.
